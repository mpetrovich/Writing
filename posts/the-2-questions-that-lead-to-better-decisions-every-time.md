There are countless decision-making frameworks out there and an equally paralyzing amount of advice on how to make good decisions. It can be overwhelming. A lot of the advice boils down to the 2 simple questions below.

![Neon light in the shape of a question mark](images/the-2-questions-that-lead-to-better-decisions-every-time/01.jpeg)

Photo by [Simone Secci](https://unsplash.com/photos/49uySSA678U)

### What problem are you trying to solve?

Crafting a solution without a clear definition of the problem can produce a solution that doesn’t actually solve the underlying problem.

Even if you might have a clear understanding of the problem, do others share the same understanding? Multiple people, each solving for a different problem, will probably not produce a solution that actually works.

Ask this question at the beginning of any decision-making process, and _document the answer_. Make sure everyone agrees with it before moving on, and frequently remind yourselves of it to avoid unproductive detours. At the end, revisit it: Does the solution actually solve the original problem?

### How do you know that?

This question cuts through assumptions and beliefs that aren’t grounded in facts. It’s incredibly versatile and can even be applied to the first question above. A few examples:

- How do you know that the problem you’re trying to solve is actually a problem? And important enough to solve? Is there a way to measure it?
- How do you know that X is the root cause of the problem? What led to X?
- How do you know that an important piece of information is true? Is it an assumption or direct observation? Is it a primary or secondary source?
- How do you know that your decision was correct? Is there a way to evaluate it?

Like the [5 whys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_whys), keep asking this question until you get an answer that’s based on direct observations or [first principles](https://fs.blog/2018/04/first-principles/).

The answer to this question can also give you a good way to measure the success of the decision. [Proactively measuring what you change](https://petro.blog/lead-time-is-the-kpi-for-process-e86a9ed045b0) is an important part of any decision-making process.
